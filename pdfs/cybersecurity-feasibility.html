Executive Summary

This feasibility study examines the opportunity for USC Upstate to launch a fully online Master of Science in CyberSecurity. The analysis draws on national workforce data, South Carolina labor market statistics, competitor program reviews, and internal cost modeling to determine whether this program is educationally sound, financially viable, and strategically aligned with the university's mission.

The short answer is yes. The United States faces a documented shortage of credentialed cybersecurity professionals that is not closing on its own.[1] South Carolina's technology sector has expanded substantially since 2020, and the Upstate region in particular lacks a public, affordable online graduate option in this field.[2] USC Upstate is well positioned to fill that gap.

Market Analysis

National Workforce Demand

The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects that employment for Information Security Analysts will grow 32 percent through 2032, a rate more than four times the national average for all occupations.[3] That figure reflects persistent pressure from several directions at once: escalating ransomware campaigns, mandatory federal compliance frameworks such as CMMC 2.0 and NIST CSF 2.0, rapid cloud migration across every industry, and the emerging use of artificial intelligence as both an attack tool and a defense mechanism.

Cybersecurity Ventures estimated that the global shortfall of qualified cybersecurity professionals reached 3.5 million open positions in 2025.[4] That number has remained stubbornly high despite years of increased program offerings, largely because the pace of hiring outstrips the supply of credentialed graduates. The (ISC)² 2025 Cybersecurity Workforce Study found that 67 percent of organizations reported having too few cybersecurity staff to respond effectively to current threats — up from 52 percent in 2022.[5]

South Carolina and Upstate Regional Demand

South Carolina's cybersecurity employment base grew 41 percent between 2020 and 2025, driven by manufacturing expansion along the BMW-Michelin-Boeing corridor, growth in financial services in Columbia and Greenville, and the enduring presence of Fort Jackson and related defense contractors in the Midlands region.[6] The South Carolina Department of Employment and Workforce tracked 1,481 active cybersecurity job postings in Q1 2026 alone, with average salary offers exceeding $115,000 — well above the state's median household income.[7]

Competitive Landscape

The online graduate cybersecurity market is competitive at the national level, but the regional picture for South Carolina is much more open. No public university in the Upstate region currently offers a fully online MS in CyberSecurity. Clemson University offers cybersecurity coursework at the undergraduate level but has not developed a dedicated graduate program in the field. The University of South Carolina Columbia offers a graduate certificate in cybersecurity but no full degree.[8]

The main competitive pressure comes from large national programs with strong brands and, in some cases, aggressive pricing. The table below summarizes the most directly relevant competitors.

SWOT Analysis

The following analysis reflects conditions as of June 2026 and draws on internal institutional data, SACSCOC requirements,[12] and comparative review of peer program development experiences.

Strengths

  • Established undergraduate CS and IT programs as natural feeder pipelines
  • USC system brand recognition throughout South Carolina
  • SACS-COC regional accreditation in good standing
  • Competitive tuition rate compared to private competitors
  • Geographic proximity to Fort Jackson and Midlands defense contractors
  • Existing Blackboard LMS reduces technology start-up cost

Weaknesses

  • No existing graduate CS enrollment base to draw on initially
  • Limited dedicated CyberSecurity faculty on current roster
  • No dedicated cyber range or research lab at present
  • Smaller national brand profile relative to Georgia Tech or Clemson
  • Significant Year 1 start-up investment required ($605,000)

Opportunities

  • No competing online public MS program in Upstate South Carolina
  • NSA Center of Academic Excellence in Cyber Defense designation available
  • Federal CHIPS Act and SC workforce development grant programs
  • Employer tuition benefit partnerships with Upstate manufacturers
  • Veterans and active-duty military enrollment via Fort Jackson pipeline
  • Greenville–Spartanburg technology corridor growth accelerating

Threats

  • Georgia Tech's $310 per credit program undercuts on price alone
  • WGU's flat-rate model appeals to highly cost-sensitive students
  • Industry salary competition makes CyberSecurity faculty hard to recruit
  • Rapid technological change requires ongoing curriculum revision investment
  • SACSCOC substantive change review adds 12–18 months to launch timeline

Closing Statement

USC Upstate stands at an inflection point. The cybersecurity profession is growing faster than the educational system is producing graduates, and South Carolina's technology employers are already feeling the shortage. This program will not solve the national problem on its own, but it will meaningfully address a local one — and in doing so, it will generate sustainable revenue, strengthen the university's graduate profile, and create real career pathways for Upstate South Carolina residents.

The window for first-mover advantage in this region will not stay open indefinitely. Moving forward in 2026 is the right call.
